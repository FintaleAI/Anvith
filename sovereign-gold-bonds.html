Why SGB

The case for SGB is really a case against physical gold.

Jewellery attracts 3% GST, 8–15% making charges, and locker rent. Gold ETFs match the price but pay no coupon. Digital gold has no sovereign backing and attracts GST. SGB is the only instrument that solves the full stack — for investors willing to hold 5–8 years.

The four exit gates

The route you choose determines the tax.

SGB has the most conditional tax treatment of any Indian retail instrument. Two routes are tax-free; two are fully taxable. This is the single most common mistake we see — investors selling SGBs on the exchange thinking maturity rules apply.

Tax-free

1. Hold to 8-year maturity

Original subscriber receives the final-day gold price (average of last 3 business days' LBMA AM fix × grams held) credited to registered bank account. Capital gain is fully exempt under Section 47(viic).

Tax-free

2. RBI premature redemption (year 5+)

Original subscriber can request redemption directly to RBI on any coupon-payment date from year 5 onwards. Proceeds credited at then-prevailing gold price. Same Section 47(viic) exemption applies.

Taxable

3. Exchange secondary sale

SGBs are listed and freely traded on NSE/BSE. Selling through the exchange breaks the Section 47(viic) wrapper. Gain is taxed as LTCG 12.5% (after 12 months) or STCG at slab.

Taxable

4. Secondary-market purchase, then maturity

If you bought the SGB on the exchange (i.e., not from a primary issue), you are not the original subscriber. Even on 8-year maturity, the gain is fully taxable as LTCG/STCG. Cost base = your exchange purchase price.

Note: The 2.5% interest coupon is fully taxable at slab rate in all four scenarios — the exemption applies only to capital gain on principal. TDS is not deducted at source on SGB interest; you must report it in the "Income from other sources" head.

Premature redemption calendar

Year-5 exit dates for active series.

If you hold a SGB series issued 5+ years ago, you are in the RBI early-redemption window. RBI permits premature redemption only on coupon payment dates; the window to submit the redemption request is 10 days before the due date. Late submissions roll to the next half-year.

Common questions

Sovereign Gold Bond FAQs.

Why is the primary tranche better than buying existing series on the exchange?
Three reasons. First: tax. Primary subscribers hold Section 47(viic) exemption on capital gains at maturity; secondary-market buyers do not. Second: price. Existing series often trade at a 1–4% premium to the underlying gold price because demand is constant and fresh supply is intermittent. Third: the ₹50/gram online discount on primary applications. If RBI notifies a tranche, we recommend primary over secondary for the full 8-year horizon investor.
Where is the bond held? Can I see it in my account?
You hold it in your Demat account (NSDL or CDSL) — just like any listed security — or as an RBI-issued physical certificate (Bond Ledger Account) if you choose. Demat is preferable: it makes secondary-market sale possible, consolidates with other holdings, and removes any risk of misplacing a paper certificate. Interest is credited directly to the bank account linked to your Demat.
How does the 2.5% interest work? Is it on my investment or on the gold value?
The coupon is 2.5% per annum on the initial investment value, not on the current gold price. So a ₹1 L subscription pays ₹2,500 per year, regardless of whether gold appreciates to ₹1.5 L or declines to ₹80,000. It is paid semi-annually, directly to your registered bank account. The interest is taxable at your slab rate.
What if I need the money before 5 years?
RBI's premature-redemption window only opens in year 5. Before that, the only exit is through the NSE/BSE secondary market — which is technically possible but typically illiquid. Spreads can be 1–3% and volumes thin. If you anticipate a potential early-exit need, do not subscribe for more than you can hold for 5+ years. For short-horizon gold exposure, a gold ETF is the better tool.
What happens if the original subscriber dies before maturity?
The nominee inherits the SGB, and the Section 47(viic) tax exemption passes with the bond — the nominee remains eligible for the tax-free maturity treatment and the RBI Y5+ redemption window. This is one of the quiet advantages of SGB for estate planning. We recommend registering a nominee at the time of subscription; if you hold in Demat, this is managed through your depository.
Can a HUF or trust invest? What are the upper limits?
Yes. Individuals can subscribe up to 4 kg (~₹37 L at current prices) per person per financial year. HUFs have the same 4 kg limit. Trusts and similar entities are permitted up to 20 kg per year. Companies cannot subscribe to SGB. The limits apply to aggregate holdings across tranches in a given fiscal year.
